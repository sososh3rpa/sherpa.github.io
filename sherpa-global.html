about us

Who Are the Sherpas?

The Sherpas are an ethnic group originally from the Kham region of eastern Tibet, who migrated across the Himalayas and settled in Nepal and later in Sikkim. The name "Sherpa" derives from the Tibetan words Shar (East) and Pa (People) — meaning "People from the East."

"Shar — East. Pa — People.
We are the People of the East."

In Sikkim, the Sherpa community is a proud and integral part of the state's rich cultural tapestry. Predominantly found in North Sikkim (Lachen, Lachung, Chungthang), East Sikkim, and West Sikkim (Yuksom), the Sherpas have maintained their distinct cultural identity, language, and Buddhist traditions for centuries.

Followers of Vajrayana Buddhism, the Sherpas of Sikkim uphold a deep spiritual life centred around monasteries (Gonpa), prayer, and community. Their vibrant festivals, distinctive dress, unique language, and warm hospitality make them one of Sikkim's most cherished communities.

history & origin

Our Heritage

The Sherpa journey is one of remarkable resilience and cultural richness — a people who carried their traditions, language, and faith across the mighty Himalayas and built a vibrant community in the green valleys of Sikkim.

~15th Century
Ancestral Roots in Kham, Tibet
Sherpa ancestors lived in the Kham region of eastern Tibet — a land of vast plateaus and Buddhist monasteries. The community was part of the broader Tibetan cultural sphere, practising Buddhism and maintaining rich oral traditions.
~16th–17th Century
Migration to Nepal & Sikkim
Sherpa clans migrated westward through the Himalayan passes, settling first in the Solu-Khumbu valley of Nepal and gradually making their way into the Kingdom of Sikkim — drawn by fertile valleys and the promise of a new home.
18th Century
Establishment of Gonpa & Community Roots
The Sherpas established their first monasteries and sacred spaces in Sikkim. Community bonds were formed through shared religious practice, festivals, and the governance of clan elders. Sherpa villages took root in North Sikkim.
19th Century
Growth Across Sikkim's Valleys
Sherpa settlements expanded across Lachen, Lachung, Chungthang in North Sikkim and into West Sikkim near Yuksom. Each village developed its own cultural rhythm, festivals, and Gonpa as spiritual centres of community life.
1947 – 1975
Indian Independence & Sikkimese Integration
With India's independence and Sikkim's eventual merger with India in 1975 as the 22nd state, the Sherpa community became Indian citizens. They participated actively in shaping Sikkim's democratic and cultural identity.
2000s – Present
Cultural Renaissance & Global Sherpa Association
Recognising the importance of preserving their unique identity, Sherpa leaders formed cultural organisations. The Sherpa Global Association was established to connect Sherpa communities across Sikkim and the world, protecting language, traditions, and heritage for future generations.
culture & traditions

Our Living Culture

👘 👘
Traditional Dress
Bakhu & Pangden
The Bakhu (or Chuba) is the traditional Sherpa robe worn by both men and women — a full-length garment of wool or brocade, fastened with a cloth belt. Women wear the colourful Pangden, a striped apron symbolising marital status and cultural identity. The Pakho hat completes ceremonial attire. During festivals, elaborate jewellery of turquoise, coral, and silver adorns the dress.

🛕 🛕
Spirituality
Vajrayana Buddhism
Buddhism is the heartbeat of Sherpa life in Sikkim. The community follows the Nyingma and Kagyu schools of Vajrayana Buddhism. Monasteries (Gonpa) serve as spiritual, social, and educational centres. Mani walls — stones inscribed with sacred mantras — line village paths. Prayer flags in five colours (Blue, White, Red, Green, Yellow) carry blessings on the wind.
Nyingma School Kagyu School Gonpa Mani Walls
🍲 🍲
Culinary Heritage
Food of the Sherpas
Sherpa cuisine reflects the Himalayan landscape — hearty, warming, and deeply nourishing. Tsampa (roasted barley flour) is the staple. Thukpa (noodle soup), Tingmo (steamed bread), and Dhindo (buckwheat porridge) are daily meals. Butter Tea (Bhoe Cha / Po Cha) — made with black tea, yak butter, and salt — is offered to every guest. Chang, a millet beer, is shared at celebrations.

🎶 🎶
Music & Arts
Sacred Music & Crafts
Sherpa cultural life is enriched by sacred music played on traditional instruments — dungchen (long horns), gyaling (oboe), damaru (drum), and cymbal. Ritual Cham dances — performed by monks in elaborate costumes and masks — tell stories of dharma and are the centrepiece of major festivals. Sherpa artisans craft intricate Thangka paintings and handwoven textiles with symbolic Buddhist motifs.

Sherpa Khä is a Tibeto-Burman language closely related to Tibetan. It is the mother tongue of the Sherpa people, carrying centuries of oral wisdom, folklore, and spiritual knowledge. The Sherpa Global Association actively runs language classes to ensure this precious tongue is passed to new generations.

festivals & celebrations

Sacred Festivals

🎊
February / March
Losar — Sherpa New Year
The most important celebration in the Sherpa calendar. Losar marks the Tibetan-Sherpa New Year with prayer ceremonies at the Gonpa, traditional dances, feasting on special foods, exchange of gifts, and the painting of doorways with auspicious symbols. Homes are decorated with prayer flags and juniper incense fills the air.
🙏
June / July
Dumji — Festival of Merit
A five-day community festival of great cultural significance, centred at the village Gonpa. Monks perform Cham dances in elaborate masks and costumes. Families contribute to a communal feast. It is considered a time to accumulate merit and strengthen community bonds through shared devotion and celebration.
🕯️
May / June
Saga Dawa — Sacred Month
The holiest month of the Tibetan Buddhist calendar, commemorating the birth, enlightenment, and parinirvana of Lord Buddha. Sherpas observe vegetarianism, perform pilgrimages to Gonpa and sacred sites, engage in extensive prayer and recitation of mantras, and practise acts of generosity and compassion.
🎭
October / November
Mani Rimdu
A three-day monastic festival featuring elaborate Cham ritual dances performed by monks wearing ornate masks representing Buddhist deities and demons. The dances symbolise the victory of dharma over ignorance. Villagers receive blessings from senior lamas and participate in sacred fire ceremonies.
💃
Varies by Monastery
Tsechu — Anniversary Dances
Celebrated on the tenth day of the Tibetan lunar month, Tsechu honours Guru Rinpoche (Padmasambhava). Monastery courtyards come alive with masked Cham dances, music from dungchen and cymbals, and the unfurling of massive sacred Thangka paintings (Thongdrol) believed to liberate those who behold them.
🌾
Seasonal
Naubarse — Harvest Thanks
An agricultural thanksgiving festival where the Sherpa community gathers to express gratitude for the harvest. Offerings of first fruits, grains, and chang are made to local deities. Traditional songs (Sherpali folk songs) are sung around communal fires, celebrating the abundance that the land provides.
